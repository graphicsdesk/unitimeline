Shafik’s Columbia: 13 months and 13 days of a campus spiraling into crisis

By Sarah Huddleston and Shea Vance

August 20, 2024

Yanchi Zheng / Illustrations Deputy

Minouche Shafik took Columbia’s helm on July 1, 2023, bringing a change the University had not seen in 21 years since Lee Bollinger became University president in 2002.

Only months into the role, however, she was confronted with protests, donor crises, political vitriol, reports of antisemitism and Islamophobia, arrests, and administrative upheaval—all of which rocked the institution as it became one of the epicenters of turmoil over the war in Gaza on college campuses across the nation.

While Shafik’s resignation came as a surprise to most, students and faculty—even those with opposing viewpoints—widely agreed that her tenure saw an erosion of trust between the administration and the University community.

Shafik’s responsibilities now fall to Katrina Armstrong, chief executive officer of the Columbia University Irving Medical Center and the new interim University president, as Shafik returns to the United Kingdom for a post with the U.K. government.

In her 13 months and 13 days as Columbia’s president, Shafik oversaw an institution spiraling into crisis. Spectator broke down Shafik’s time at the University, from the day of her presidential announcement to her resignation.

Jan. 18, 2023

Jonathan Lavine, then-chair of the board of trustees, announced to the Columbia community that Shafik would succeed Bollinger in July, making her the first woman and first person of color to serve as the institution’s top administrator.

With a background in economics, a presidency at the London School of Economics, and the title of baroness in the House of Lords, Shafik was initially met with excitement from faculty, students, and local politicians.

Attendees congratulate Shafik on her appointment as Columbia’s 20th president in Pulitzer Hall on Jan. 18, 2023. Photo by Pippa Tsuki Carlson / Senior Staff Photographer

“We have an opportunity today to make extraordinary contributions to the world,” Shafik said at her appointment event. “I’m looking forward to getting to know all of you, the extraordinary people who inhabit this extraordinary University, in what we all know is the most extraordinary city in the world.”

June 29, 2023

The Supreme Court struck down affirmative action, a legal precedent that Bollinger had helped build as the named defendant in the 2003 Supreme Court case Grutter v. Bollinger. Columbia published a statement on the reversal, affirming that diversity is “central” to the institution’s identity.

July 3, 2023

Shafik stepped into the Office of the President after her tenure officially began on July 1, greeting the community with a video message explaining that her “primary focus in the coming months will be getting to know you better and to listening to your Columbia stories.”

She ended the address with a promise—“I’ll see you on College Walk.”

Aug. 23, 2o23

Shafik issued her first public comments on affirmative action as president, announcing a series of initiatives that included establishing an admissions working group and assigning a team to focus on creating opportunities for students from local public schools, community colleges, and public institutions.

“I promise a sustained commitment, including securing the resources necessary for meaningful impact, to ensure Columbia remains a beacon for generations of future students,” Shafik wrote.

The group was set to issue a set of recommendations in December 2023 but has yet to publicly release any findings.

Aug. 27, 2023

Spectator spoke to Shafik during the 2023 New Student Orientation Program, where she discussed the conversations she had been having with stakeholders amid her transition into the job. During a tour of campus as first-years moved in, she met and chatted with orientation leaders and new students.

Shafik greets orientation leaders during first-year move-in on Aug. 27, 2023. Photo by Judy Goldstein / Senior Staff Photographer

Shafik discussed her approach to managing times of crisis, noting how her experience at the International Monetary Fund and the Department for International Development had prepared her for such leadership.

“You just need to be calm, you need to be systematic, you need to reassure people,” she said.

Shafik has not interviewed with Spectator since, despite numerous requests.

Oct. 4, 2023

Shafik was inaugurated on the steps of Low Library in a ceremony attended by local leaders, Columbia administrators, faculty, staff, and students.

For the first time, Shafik formally presented her vision for Columbia as a global institution empowered by its foundations of humanist education. In her address, she brought forward themes from her book, “What We Owe Each Other: A New Social Contract for a Better Society.”

Shafik delivers her inaugural address on Oct. 4, 2023. Photo by Gabriella Gregor-Splaver / Senior Staff Photographer

“What I would like to offer is leadership that provides not only nourishment, but cross-pollination, promoting creativity and flowering in all its forms in our great institution,” Shafik said. “And I ask of you, as part of this new social contract, to engage wholeheartedly in becoming leaders, developing ideas that matter, and engaging locally and globally.”

The sparsely attended ceremony was disrupted throughout by three separate groups of protesters—members of the Columbia Postdoctoral Workers-United Auto Workers making demands for a second contract; Sunrise Movement at Columbia protesting investments in fossil fuels and fossil fuel-funded research; and Columbia University Irving Medical Center students in white coats, who protested the University’s response to decades of sexual abuse by former Columbia gynecologist Robert Hadden.

Students protest the University’s handling of the Robert Hadden case during Shafik’s inauguration on Oct. 4, holding signs reading “Columbia protected a predator OBGYN for 25 years.” Photo by Genevieve Depke / Staff Photographer

Armstrong and Shafik had apologized two weeks earlier to Hadden survivors after a bombshell report alleged that Columbia “never fully accounted for its role” in Hadden’s abuse. The administrators later announced a third-party investigation, among other initiatives.

Several speakers acknowledged the protesters during their addresses at Shafik’s inauguration, including board of trustees co-chair Claire Shipman, CC ’86, SIPA ’94, who said, “We welcome all voices at all times, especially voices of dissent.”

“You will not have a Columbia welcome without protest—it would not be authentic—so get used to it,” First Deputy Mayor of New York City Sheena Wright, CC ’90, Law ’94, said.

Oct. 9, 2023

Shafik issued her first statement on the escalating violence in Israel and Gaza two days after Hamas’s attack on Israel, in which Hamas militants killed nearly 1,200 people and took around 250 civilian hostages. Israel quickly declared war on Hamas after the attack and began carrying out airstrikes and a siege of Gaza.

“I was devastated by the horrific attack on Israel this weekend and the ensuing violence that is affecting so many people,” Shafik wrote. “Little is certain except that the fighting and human suffering are not likely to end soon.”

Oct. 11, 2023

The New York Police Department said it was investigating an alleged assault against an Israeli School of General Studies student—a case that garnered widespread media attention and first thrust Columbia into the national spotlight. The Manhattan district attorney initially charged the incident as a hate crime but moved to drop the case in late June.

Oct. 12, 2023

Hundreds of protesters packed South Lawn, with the Columbia chapters of Students for Justice in Palestine and Jewish Voice for Peace demonstrating opposite to Students Supporting Israel at Columbia. The University restricted the historically open Morningside campus to Columbia ID holders prior to the protest, marking the semester’s first such limitation in relation to demonstrations, and allowed the NYPD on campus.

Hundreds of demonstrators flood South Lawn on Oct. 12, 2023, for a protest organized by SJP and JVP and a counterprotest organized by SSI. Photo by Pippa Tsuki Carlson / Senior Staff Photographer

Oct. 23, 2023

Columbia indefinitely postponed its annual Giving Day after “careful consideration and consultation with University and alumni leadership.” Donors soon began pulling funding from Columbia as protests picked up, with billionaire Leon Cooperman, Business ’67, telling Fox Business days later that he would no longer be donating to the University.

Oct. 25, 2023

Accuracy in Media deployed its first “doxxing truck” to the Morningside campus, displaying the names and faces of affiliates who were allegedly members of student groups that signed onto a Palestinian solidarity statement.

Students cover the faces and names displayed on the truck in an anti-doxxing protest on Oct.25, 2o23. Photo by Erick Berlanga / Staff Photographer

Nov. 1, 2023

Shafik, Barnard President Laura Rosenbury, and Teachers College President Thomas Bailey announced the creation of a task force on antisemitism, writing that antisemitism “has been rising here in New York, across the country, and around the world in recent years.” Jewish and Israeli affiliates had reported feeling unsafe on campus, experiencing in-person or online harassment, and feeling compelled to hide their Jewish identity.

The University simultaneously established the Doxing Resource Group to help students who had their personal information published online. Beyond the doxxing trucks that had visited campus, Pro-Palestinian students had reported having their names and personal information added to doxxing spreadsheets and websites, as well as experiencing verbal harassment.

Nov. 10, 2023

Then-Senior Executive Vice President Gerald Rosberg announced that SJP and JVP had been suspended through the fall term, citing “threatening rhetoric and intimidation” and an “unauthorized” walkout the groups organized that day. The suspension remains in place over nine months later.

Demonstrators pack Low Steps for an art-centered pro-Palestinian protest on Nov. 10, 2023. Photo by Gabriella Gregor-Splaver / Senior Staff Photographer

Spectator first reported that senior administrators had unilaterally revised the University events policies 17 days before citing them in the suspensions of SJP and JVP. The revisions handed Columbia the “sole discretion” to determine “final and not appealable” sanctions on student groups and their individual members.

Rosberg publicly confirmed the changes at a November 2023 University Senate plenary, saying that the administration was committed to reviewing the policy.

The administration faced scrutiny for the suspensions from over 20 elected officials in New York state and hundreds of affiliates. The New York Civil Liberties Union and Palestine Legal filed a lawsuit in March contesting the suspensions.

Nov. 14, 2023

Organizers relaunched Columbia University Apartheid Divest, a collective of over 80 student groups that signed on to several demands, including the University’s divestment from Israel and the reinstatement of SJP and JVP. Columbia students first formed CUAD in 2016.

Nov. 16, 2023

The U.S. Department of Education opened a federal investigation into Columbia and several peer institutions over alleged violations of Title VI of the 1964 Civil Rights Act relating to antisemitic and anti-Muslim harassment—the first in a long line of federal inquiries into the University.

Dec. 5, 2023

Then-Harvard University President Claudine Gay and then-University of Pennsylvania President Liz Magill, alongside Massachusetts Institute of Technology President Sally Kornbluth, testified before the House Committee on Education and the Workforce.

Gay and Magill later resigned from their positions amid backlash to what critics characterized as weak responses to the committee’s questions on whether calling for the genocide of Jewish people violates their campuses’ policies.

The committee had also invited Shafik to testify but she declined, citing a scheduling conflict. Two days after the hearing, Columbia updated its Event Policy and Campus Resources FAQ page to clarify that calls for genocide against Jewish people violate the University’s code of conduct.

Dec. 20, 2023

Shafik and Dennis Mitchell, executive vice president for University Life and then the interim University provost, launched four initiatives designed to address “bigotry, intimidation, and harassment” at Columbia. Among them was a review, conducted alongside the University Senate, of events policies and the Rules of University Conduct.

Jan. 18, 2024

Shafik named Angela Olinto as the University’s new provost. Olinto hailed from the University of Chicago, where she served on its committee on freedom of expression.

Jan. 19, 2024

CUAD, the Barnard-Columbia Abolition Collective, and Student-Worker Solidarity organized a “divestment now” demonstration on Low Steps, demanding the University divest from companies with ties to Israel and reiterating calls for a tuition strike.

Students rally for divestment on Low Steps on Jan. 19 under a flurry of snow. Photo by Gabriella Gregor-Splaver / Senior Staff Photographer

Nearly two dozen students reported a foul smell, physical symptoms, or property damage after the protest, and several students sought medical care. Two days after the rally, Public Safety announced an investigation alongside local and federal authorities into the incident. The University banned the “alleged perpetrators” from campus following “what appear to have been serious crimes, possibly hate crimes,” Mitchell wrote in an email to the Columbia community.

Jan. 30, 2024

Shafik named Cas Holloway as the University’s first chief operating officer. Holloway had no experience working in higher education prior to his appointment at Columbia, instead bringing a decadeslong career in government and business. He served as deputy mayor of operations for New York City under former Mayor Mike Bloomberg in 2011.

Feb. 12, 2024

The House Committee of Education and Workforce launched a probe into Columbia, demanding a series of documents, communications, and reports since Jan. 1, 2021, that concern “antisemitic acts or incidents.”

The same day, a Jewish student filed a lawsuit alleging the University violated Title VI of the Civil Rights Act of 1964. As the academic year progressed, Columbia was hit with several more Title VI lawsuits, which alleged both antisemitism and anti-Palestinian discrimination.

Feb. 19, 2024

Columbia released an interim demonstration policy restricting protests to designated demonstration times and areas: on South Lawn or at the Sundial, from 12 p.m. to 6 p.m. on weekdays. The policy also outlined separate, three-tiered disciplinary processes for student groups and individuals accused of violating the policy—a move that drew criticism from University senators.

At a Feb. 23 University Senate plenary, Shafik said that she believes administrators should maintain a position of neutrality because otherwise “it constrains the freedom of speech of faculty and students.”

Feb. 29, 2024

Columbia’s Advisory Committee on Socially Responsible Investing rejected CUAD’s proposal to divest from Israel, citing a failure to “meet the broad consensus test required for consideration of divestment.” The committee wrote that consensus is “a purposefully high bar” as the University has a “general aversion to using divestment for political purposes.”

March 11, 2024

The House Committee on Education and the Workforce announced that Shafik would in April testify in a hearing titled “Columbia in Crisis: Columbia University's Response to Antisemitism,” alongside board of trustees co-chairs Shipman and David Greenwald, Law ’83.

March 24, 2024

Organizers held an event titled “Resistance 101” in an off-campus residence hall after both Barnard and Columbia declined to authorize it as an on-campus event. During the event, panelist Khaled Barakat—who is allegedly a member of the Popular Front for the Liberation of Palestine, a U.S.-designated terrorist organization—referred to members of Hamas, the Islamic Jihad, and the PFLP as his “friends and brothers.”

Four days later, Columbia launched an investigation into the event, notified law enforcement, banned the speakers from campus, and engaged an outside firm to investigate.

April 3, 2024

Columbia suspended and evicted multiple students it identified as participating in the “Resistance 101” panel.

“I did not become a university president to punish students,” Shafik wrote in an email two days later. “At the same time, actions like this on our campus must have consequences. That I would ever have to declare the following is in itself surprising, but I want to make clear that it is absolutely unacceptable for any member of this community to promote the use of terror or violence.”

April 14, 2024

Ahead of Shafik’s testimony before Congress, the University announced that campus access would be heavily restricted throughout the week due to anticipated “activities that may impact campus.”

April 17, 2024

Dozens of pro-Palestinian demonstrators established the “Gaza Solidarity Encampment” on the east side of South Lawn at around 4 a.m., pitching tents and demanding the University divest from Israel.

Demonstrators pitch tents in the early hours of April 17, labeling the encampment the “Liberated Zone.” Photo by Columbia Daily Spectator

At 10:17 a.m., Shafik began her nearly three-and-a-half-hour testimony before Congress alongside Shipman and Greenwell, as well as Antisemitism Task Force co-chair David Schizer—a last-minute addition to the lineup.

Shafik focused on how the University was disciplining its students for unauthorized events and demonstrations. When asked if a call for genocide against Jewish people violates Columbia’s code of conduct, all witnesses answered swiftly: “Yes, it does.”

Shafik, Schizer, Shipman, and Greenwell testify on antisemitism at Columbia before Congress on April 17. Photo by Gabriella Gregor-Splaver / Senior Staff Photographer

The committee’s questioning—led by Rep. Elise Stefanik (R-N.Y.) and committee chair Rep. Virginia Foxx (R-N.C.)—centered largely around Columbia’s enforcement of its campus protest policies. Representatives pressured Shafik to provide information on faculty disciplinary proceedings, which is typically kept confidential, and demanded that multiple faculty members be removed from their positions.

As Shafik fielded questions from the committee and promised to continue disciplining affiliates who violate University policy, the developing situation on campus started to garner media attention. After the hearing concluded, protesters began receiving paper notices warning that if they did not vacate the lawn, they would be suspended.

The University had suspended a total of 15 students in relation to “antisemitic incidents,” Shafik told the committee. That number would soon climb.

April 18, 2024

Shafik wrote a letter to the NYPD announcing that all students remaining in the encampment had been suspended and authorizing police to sweep the encampment. Shafik consulted the University Senate’s executive committee prior to her decision but did not receive their approval.

NYPD officers arrest over 100 protesters in the “Gaza Solidarity Encampment” and load them into correctional buses headed to One Police Plaza. Photo by Jessica Samudio / Senior Staff Photographer

Thirty-three hours after students launched the first encampment, the NYPD made over 100 arrests on campus, the largest mass arrest at Columbia since 1968.

After police filled correctional buses with encampment demonstrators, many of those watching the arrests unfold hopped the fence to the opposite side of South Lawn, establishing the second iteration of the “Gaza Solidarity Encampment.”

Columbia leadership immediately faced intense backlash over the authorization of the sweep, with students, faculty, staff, alumni, politicians, donors, and First Amendment groups decrying the arrests as a violation of protesters’ rights. Another faction supported the move, praising Shafik for what they said was a strong stance against antisemitism.

April 21, 2024

Rabbi Elie Buechler, director of the Orthodox Union-Jewish Learning Initiative on Campus at Columbia/Barnard, advised Jewish students to “return home as soon as possible” after several reports of antisemitic incidents on and around campus had surfaced the night prior.

In front of the Sundial the evening of April 20, an individual held a sign reading “Al-Qasam’s Next Targets” with an arrow pointing at a group of pro-Israel counterprotesters. Al-Qassam is the military wing of Hamas.

Other Jewish students were told to “go back to Poland” at a protest, according to a video reviewed by Spectator, and another student reported having an object thrown at him while counterprotesting a pro-Palestinian demonstration outside the campus gates.

April 22, 2024

Shafik announced a same-day shift to virtual classes as a means to “deescalate the rancor” and “give us all a chance to consider next steps.” That evening, Olinto shared with the community that classes would be hybrid for the remainder of the semester.

Columbia College students also passed a nonbinding referendum on divestment from Israel, with nearly 77 percent in favor and roughly 40 percent voter participation. About 68 percent and 66 percent of voters, respectively, were in favor of canceling the opening of the Tel Aviv Global Center and the University’s dual degree program with Tel Aviv University.

April 24, 2024

House Speaker Mike Johnson (R-La.) held a press conference on Low Steps alongside Foxx and demanded Shafik step down.

House Speaker Mike Johnson (R-La.) holds a press conference with four other Republican representatives on Low Steps on April 24. Photo by Gabriella Gregor-Splaver / Senior Staff Photographer

The same day, the board of trustees issued a statement backing Shafik’s leadership and expressing strong support for the president “as she steers the university through this extraordinarily challenging time.”

April 29, 2024

Negotiations to end the encampment fell apart when Shafik announced in the morning that Columbia “will not divest from Israel,” officially ending a week of talks between members of the “Gaza Solidarity Encampment” and administrators. Shafik urged protesters to “voluntarily disperse” and said University administrators were considering “alternative internal options to end this crisis.”

That afternoon, the University began distributing notices giving protesters until 2 p.m. to clear the encampment, warning that those remaining would be suspended.

Hundreds of demonstrators picket around the encampment on April 29 as participating students braced for suspensions after negotiations officially ended. Photo by Stella Ragas / Senior Staff Photographer

As the deadline passed, hundreds of affiliates picketed around South Lawn in one of the largest protests the campus had seen in months. Organizers urged students to protect the encampment.

April 30, 2024

At around midnight, dozens of masked protesters broke into Hamilton Hall, barricading the doors, breaking windows, unfurling banners, and promising to remain until Columbia met their demands of divestment from Israel, financial transparency, and amnesty for protesters. Protesters renamed the building “Hind’s Hall” in honor of Hind Rajab, a six-year-old Palestinian girl killed by the Israeli military in Gaza. Shafik’s administration was once again at a turning point.

Occupants drop banners from Hamilton Hall within minutes of entering the building around midnight on April 30. Photo by Stella Ragas / Senior Staff Photographer

Columbia placed campus under a near-total lockdown, only allowing access to essential personnel and students living in on-campus dormitories. A University spokesperson announced that all students occupying Hamilton would be expelled as the protesters drew the attention of the White House, which condemned the occupation and protesters’ use of the word “intifada.”

[Read more: In Focus: When Hamilton Hall became ‘Hind’s Hall’]

Congressional Democrats wrote a letter to the board of trustees, expressing disappointment that “despite promises to do so, Columbia University has not yet disbanded the unauthorized and impermissible encampment of anti-Israel, anti-Jewish activists on campus.”

Less than 24 hours after the occupation began, Shafik once again authorized an NYPD sweep of the “Gaza Solidarity Encampment” and occupied Hamilton. Hundreds of police in riot gear, who pushed reporters and onlookers off campus and into University buildings, swarmed campus and made over 100 arrests. Officers deployed stun grenades, used an electric saw, slammed protesters with metal gates, and threw an individual down the stairs. One accidentally fired a gun inside the building.

Police flood campus and the surrounding area less than 24 hours after the occupation began, blocking off streets and pushing onlookers into surrounding buildings. Photo by Stella Ragas / Senior Staff Photographer

Multiple students sought medical attention, with the NYPD reportedly breaking one individual’s orbital bone. In addition to authorizing the sweep, Shafik asked police to maintain a continued presence on campus for at least another two weeks.

May 1, 2024

Shafik wrote to the Columbia community the day after the sweep of Hamilton, noting that her “first responsibility is safety” and thanking the police “for their incredible professionalism and support.”

The University later announced that all final exams would be moved online.

May 3, 2024

Two days after her initial statement, Shafik addressed the community in a video posted to Instagram.

“These past two weeks have been among the most difficult in Columbia’s history,” Shafik said. “The turmoil and tension, division and disruption, have impacted the entire community. You, our students, have paid an especially high price.”

Campus remained largely locked down through the end of the academic year, with restrictions gradually lifting for select affiliates.

May 6, 2024

Columbia canceled its Universitywide Commencement ceremonies and moved most school-specific Class Days off-campus. The decision followed several messages from the University stressing the urgency of dismantling the encampment to allow Commencement to occur on campus. The University had already set up bleachers on Low Steps and made preparations for the ceremonies.

May 9, 2024

The Union Theological Seminary’s board of trustees endorsed divestment from “companies substantially and intractably benefiting from the war in Palestine,” becoming one of the first institutions of higher education to announce such plans. UTS has a partnership with Columbia and holds its faculty of theology, but has a separate endowment worth $110 million.

May 15, 2024

Greenwald and Shipman publicly backed Shafik’s leadership in response to a Washington Post article describing a “bunker mentality” within Columbia’s top ranks.

May 16, 2024

The Faculty of Arts and Sciences passed a vote of no confidence in Shafik, with 65 percent of participating faculty in favor. The Columbia chapter of the American Association of University Professors had called for the vote following Shafik’s authorization of the April 30 NYPD sweep.

May 31, 2024

Dozens of pro-Palestinian protesters occupied South Lawn once more, repitching an encampment in time for the University’s annual alumni weekend festivities. The “Revolt for Rafah” installation again demanded that Columbia cut its financial ties with Israel and provide full financial transparency of its direct and indirect investments, and called on alumni to withhold their donations.

Low Library stands illuminated for alumni weekend on May 31, welcoming back former students against a backdrop of protest. Photo by Gaby Diaz / Senior Staff Photographer

June 2, 2024

As alumni weekend came to a close, protesters voluntarily disbanded—but left behind strips of a tent’s tarp walls spray-painted with the messages “We’ll be back bitches” and “Long live the intifada.”

Demonstrators disbanded the third installment of the “Gaza Solidarity Encampment” on June 2, but promised a return in spray-painted messages. Photo by Gaby Diaz / Senior Staff Photographer

June 20, 2024

Columbia College Dean Josef Sorett informed his staff that three administrators were placed on leave as the University investigated an incident involving leaked text messages the deans sent during a panel on Jewish student life.

Sorett, former Vice Dean and Chief Administrative Officer Susan Chang-Kim, former Dean of Undergraduate Student Life Cristen Kromm, and former Associate Dean for Student and Family Support Matthew Patashnick all participated in the exchange. Sorett, who participated in the exchange to a lesser extent than the other deans, apologized for his role in an email to alumni.

One text described the panel as “difficult to listen to,” and another included vomit emojis while referring to a Spectator op-ed written by a campus rabbi.

July 1, 2024

Gerald Rosberg retired from his post as Columbia’s senior executive vice president.

July 8, 2024

Chang-Kim, Kromm, and Patashnick were permanently removed from their positions following the House Committee on Education and the Workforce’s publication of the full exchange after demanding Columbia hand the texts over.

“This incident revealed behavior and sentiments that were not only unprofessional, but also, disturbingly touched on ancient antisemitic tropes,” Shafik wrote in an email to the community.

August 8, 2024

Chang-Kim, Kromm, and Patashnick resigned from the University.

August 14, 2024

Shafik resigned effective immediately, ending a turbulent tenure at the helm of a University pressured by lawmakers, faculty, and students across a spectrum of viewpoints. She will return to the United Kingdom, chairing “a review of the government’s approach to international development” at a time when the government has grown increasingly critical of Israel.

The decision was sudden for students and faculty alike and came only days before NSOP and the start of the 2024-25 academic year.

Katrina Armstrong, CEO of the medical center, was appointed as interim president. No stranger to leadership in times of crisis, Armstrong will be tasked with rebuilding a Columbia that, to many, appears divided beyond repair.
